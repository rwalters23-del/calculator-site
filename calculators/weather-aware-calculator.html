Why Humidity Increases AC Costs

Your AC doesn't just cool your home — it removes humidity too. Higher humidity means your AC works harder because it's removing moisture from the air, not just lowering temperature.

  • Temperature difference: The bigger the gap between outside and inside temps, the more energy your AC uses.
  • Humidity matters: Each pound of water removed from the air takes about 1,000 BTUs of energy.
  • SEER ratings: Modern AC units are rated by Seasonal Energy Efficiency Ratio (SEER). Higher SEER = lower costs.

How We Calculate

We estimate your AC's hourly energy usage based on:

  • BTU output: 1 ton of cooling = 12,000 BTUs
  • Efficiency factor: Assumes a SEER 14 rating (typical for modern systems)
  • Load factor: Adjusts for current weather conditions
  • Real weather data: Uses your location's actual temperature and humidity

Tips to Lower Your AC Bill

  • Use a smart thermostat: Program it to raise temp when you're away
  • Keep filters clean: A clogged filter can increase energy use by 5-15%
  • Seal air leaks: Prevent cool air from escaping
  • Use ceiling fans: They make rooms feel 4°F cooler
  • Schedule maintenance: A well-maintained AC runs more efficiently

Common Questions

How accurate is this calculator?

This uses real weather data, so it's more accurate than calculators that use average values. However, actual costs vary based on your home's insulation, AC efficiency, and local electricity rates.

What size AC do I need?

Generally, you need about 1 ton of cooling per 500-600 square feet. But factors like ceiling height, climate, and sun exposure matter too.

Why does humidity affect cost so much?

When humidity is high, your AC spends energy removing moisture from the air. On a humid 90°F day, your AC might use 30% more energy than on a dry 90°F day.
